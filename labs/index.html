Frequently asked

The questions we always get.

What's the difference between Incremento Labs and Incremento Studio?

The Studio builds websites and runs marketing. Labs builds software and AI systems. Most clients start with one and discover they need the other - a site that captures enquiries is Studio work; the system that reads those enquiries, flags the urgent ones and drafts a first reply is Labs. Same people, same standards, different problem.

What does the Labs Sprint cost?

It's fixed-scope and fixed-fee, and the fee depends on what the workflow actually is - a document-extraction pipeline and a customer-facing agent aren't the same build. You get the number after the first conversation and before anything starts, so there's no meter running and no surprise invoice.

Do we need our data in order first?

Usually not as much as people fear. Part of the audit is finding out what state your data is actually in - and it's rarely as bad as you think or as good as the vendor hoped. If the honest answer is that the data isn't there yet, we'll tell you that instead of building something that pretends otherwise.

Isn't this just ChatGPT with extra steps?

For some tasks, honestly, yes - and if a chat subscription solves your problem we'll say so and save you the engagement. What it can't do is read your systems, act inside your workflows, answer from your own documents rather than guessing, or run reliably at volume with evals behind it. That gap between a demo and production is most of the work.

Where does our data go?

Wherever you decide, and that decision should be deliberate. Some tasks can run against a hosted model API; some shouldn't leave your infrastructure. We'll tell you which is which, and build so you're not handing client data to a third party by accident. GDPR isn't optional, and neither is knowing where the data sits.

How do we know it's actually working?

Because we measure it before and after, and we build the evals as part of the project rather than as an afterthought. Hours saved, error rates, response times - whatever the task was. If it isn't beating the thing it replaced, it gets switched off. AI projects fail most often because nobody defined what success looked like before they started.
